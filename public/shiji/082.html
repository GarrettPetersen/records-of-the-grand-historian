Tian Dan was a distant relative of the Qi ruling family. During King Min's reign, Dan served as a market official in Linzi but was not recognized. When Yan sent Yue Yi to attack and defeat Qi, King Min of Qi fled, and afterward defended the city of Ju. The Yan army drove deep and pacified Qi, while Tian Dan fled to Anping and ordered his clansmen to cut the ends of their cart axles and attach iron collars. Soon after, the Yan army attacked Anping. The city fell, and the Qi people fled in panic, jamming the roads. Their broken carts caused defeat, and they were captured by Yan. Only Tian Dan's clansmen escaped thanks to the iron collars, and they defended Jimo to the east. Yan had taken all the Qi cities in surrender, but only Ju and Jimo held out. The Yan army heard the Qi king was in Ju and combined their forces to attack it. After Nao Chi killed King Min in Ju, he held firm defense and resisted the Yan army for several years without falling. Yan led troops east to surround Jimo. The governor of Jimo went out to fight and was defeated and killed. The people in the city together chose Tian Dan, saying: 'In the Anping battle, Tian Dan's clansmen survived intact thanks to the iron collars. He is skilled with troops.' They established him as general to defend Jimo against Yan.

Soon after, King Zhao of Yan died, and King Hui ascended. He had a falling out with Yue Yi. Tian Dan heard this and released reverse spies in Yan, proclaiming: 'The Qi king is already dead. Only two cities remain unconquered. Yue Yi fears execution and dares not return home. Using the campaign against Qi as a pretext, he really wants to turn his troops south and become king of Qi. The Qi people are not yet attached to him, so he is delaying the attack on Jimo to wait for developments. What the Qi people fear is only that another general might come - then Jimo would be finished.' The Yan king believed this and sent Qi Jie to replace Yue Yi.

Yue Yi then fled to Zhao, and the Yan officials and soldiers were furious. Tian Dan then ordered the people in the city to sacrifice to their ancestors in the courtyards whenever they ate. Flying birds all gathered and ate the food in the city. The Yan people found this strange. Tian Dan then proclaimed: 'A spirit has come down to teach me.' He then told the people in the city: 'A divine person should become my teacher.' One soldier said: 'Could I be the teacher?' He then turned and ran away. Tian Dan then rose, stepped back, sat facing east, and served him as teacher. The soldier said: 'I deceived you, my lord. I truly have no abilities.' Tian Dan said: 'Don't say that!' He then treated him as teacher. Whenever he went out to give orders, he always referred to him as the divine teacher. He then proclaimed: 'I only fear that the Yan army will cut off the noses of captured Qi soldiers and place them in the front ranks to fight against us - then Jimo would be defeated.' The Yan people heard this and did as he said. The people in the city saw that all the surrendered Qi soldiers had their noses cut off. They were all furious and defended firmly, fearing only capture. Dan again released reverse spies saying: 'I fear the Yan people will dig up the tombs and graves outside our city and mutilate our ancestors - it chills my heart.' The Yan army dug up all the tombs and burned the corpses. The people of Jimo saw this from the city walls. They all wept tears and wished to go out and fight, their anger increased tenfold.

Tian Dan knew his soldiers were now usable. He personally carried building materials, shared the labor with his soldiers, had his wives and concubines join the ranks, and distributed all food and drink to feast the soldiers. He ordered all the armored soldiers to hide, had old men, weaklings, and women defend the city walls, and sent envoys to negotiate surrender with Yan. The Yan army all shouted 'Long live!' Tian Dan again collected gold from the people, getting a thousand yi. He had Jimo's rich merchants send it to the Yan general, saying: 'Jimo will surrender immediately. Please don't plunder our clans, families, wives, and concubines, and let us dwell in peace.' The Yan general was greatly pleased and promised this. The Yan army became increasingly lax as a result.

Tian Dan then collected over a thousand oxen from the city. He clothed them in crimson silk painted with five-colored dragon patterns, bound weapons to their horns, poured oil on their tails with bundled reeds, and set them on fire. He drilled dozens of holes in the city wall. At night he released the oxen, with five thousand strong warriors following behind. The oxen tails burned hot, and they charged angrily at the Yan army. The Yan army was greatly startled in the night. The torch fires on the oxen tails were bright and dazzling. The Yan army saw them as dragon patterns, and all they touched died or were injured. The five thousand then attacked silently (with wooden gags in their mouths), while the city drums roared in support. The old and weak all beat bronze vessels to make noise, shaking heaven and earth. The Yan army was greatly terrified and fled in defeat. The Qi people then annihilated and killed their general Qi Jie. The Yan army fled in confusion. The Qi people pursued the defeated and chased the fleeing. Every city they passed rebelled against Yan and returned to Tian Dan. His troops increased daily. Riding the victory, Yan suffered defeat after defeat until they reached the Yellow River. All seventy-plus Qi cities were recovered. They then welcomed King Xiang from Ju, entered Linzi, and he took up governance.

Grand Historian says: Warfare engages with the orthodox but wins with the extraordinary. Those skilled at it produce inexhaustible extraordinary tactics. Extraordinary and orthodox tactics give birth to each other, like a ring without end. Indeed, at the beginning like a virgin, when the enemy opens the door; Later like an escaped rabbit, the enemy cannot block it: this is what is meant by Tian Dan!

Initially, after Nao Chi killed King Min, the people of Ju sought King Min's son Fa Zhang. They found him in Grand Historian Tao's home, working as a gardener. Tao's daughter pitied him and treated him well. Later Fa Zhang secretly confessed his feelings to the girl, and she then had relations with him. When the people of Ju together established Fa Zhang as Qi king to resist Yan from Ju, Grand Historian Tao's daughter then became queen - hence the saying 'Jun Wang Hou' (the gardener became king, and the gardener's daughter became queen).

When Yan first entered Qi, they heard that Wang Juan of Hua yi was worthy. They ordered the army: 'Surround Hua yi for thirty li and do not enter,' because of Wang Juan. Soon after, they sent someone to tell Juan: 'The Qi people greatly esteem your righteousness. I will make you a general and enfeoff you with ten thousand households.' Juan firmly declined. The Yan messenger said: 'If you don't listen, I will lead the three armies and slaughter Hua yi.' Wang Juan said: 'A loyal minister does not serve two lords; a chaste woman does not take two husbands. The Qi king did not listen to my advice, so I retreated and farmed in the countryside. Now that the state has been destroyed, I could not preserve it; Now you coerce me with troops to become your general - this is helping the tyrant commit atrocities. Rather than live without righteousness, I would certainly prefer to be boiled alive!' He then hung his neck from a tree branch and strangled himself to death. Qi's exiled grandees heard this and said: 'Wang Juan was just a commoner, yet his righteousness would not allow him to serve Yan. How much more so for those of us who hold office and receive salaries!' They then gathered together like those in Ju, sought out various royal descendants, and established Xiang as king.
