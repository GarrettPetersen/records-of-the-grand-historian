The Xia Shu says: Yu controlled the floodwaters for thirteen years, passing by his home but not entering the gate. On land he traveled by cart, on water by boat, through mud he stepped on rushes, through mountains he used bridges. To distinguish the nine provinces, following the mountains he dredged the rivers, according to the soil he established tributes. He opened nine roads, dammed nine marshes, and crossed nine mountains. Yet the river disasters spread and overflowed, harming China especially severely. He made only this his task. Therefore he guided the river from Ji Shi through Long Men, south to Hua Yin, east down to Di Zhu, and Meng Jin, Luo Rui, until Da Pei. Therefore Yu thought that the river's source was high, the water turbulent and fierce, difficult to flow on flat land, frequently causing breaches, so he made two channels to divert the river. He diverted it north to high ground, passing through Jiang Shui to the mainland, spreading it into nine rivers, all flowing counter to the river, entering the Bohai Sea. The nine rivers were dredged, the nine marshes drained, the various Xia were peaceful, and the merit extended to the Three Dynasties.

Gnawing mulberry floats, Huai and Si full; long not returning, water only slow.' Another said: 'The river boils and rushes gurgling; north crossing the mire, dredging the flow is difficult.' Pluck long reeds and sink beautiful jade; the River Earl promised, but firewood is not continuous.' Firewood not continuous, the Wei people's crime; burn desolate, sigh, what to control the water with!' Bundle forest bamboo and stake stone dikes; Xuan Fang blocked, ten thousand blessings come.' Therefore they finally blocked Hulu Zi; built a palace on it, named Xuan Fang Palace. And led north along the Yellow River with two canals, restored Yu's old traces; and the lands of Liang and Chu again became peaceful, without water disasters.

From this time on, those in power competed to speak of water benefits. Shuo Fang, Xi He, He Xi, Jiu Quan all led the Yellow River and stream valleys to irrigate fields; And Guan Zhong's Fu Canal and Ling Zhi led Du water; Ru Nan and Jiu Jiang led the Huai; Dong Hai led Ju Ding; Below Tai Shan led Wen water: all dug canals for irrigating fields, each over ten thousand qing. Other small canals that split mountains and opened roads cannot all be spoken of. Yet the most famous of them was in Xuan Fang.

The Grand Historian said: I south ascended Lu Shan, viewed how Yu dredged the nine Jiang, then arrived at Hui Ji Tai Huang; ascended Gu Su, gazed at the five lakes; East I peered at Luo Rui and Da Pi, met the Yellow River, traveled the Huai, Si, Ji, and Ta Luo canals; West I gazed at Shu's Min Shan and Li Dui; North from Long Men to Shuo Fang. He said: 'How extreme are water's benefits and harms!' I followed carrying firewood to block Xuan Fang; grieved by the Hulu Zi poem, I made the Yellow River Canals book.
