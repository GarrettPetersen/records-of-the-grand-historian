The ways of humanity are woven in countless patterns, and rules and standards penetrate everywhere. People are guided forward with benevolence and righteousness, restrained with punishments. Thus those of great virtue hold honored positions, those with heavy stipends enjoy glorious favor. This serves to unify the realm and bring order to the multitudes. The human body finds ease in riding and driving, so for it are made golden carriages with inlaid yokes to multiply its adornments; The eyes delight in the five colors, so for them are made embroidered patterned textiles to display their brilliance; The ears take pleasure in bells and chimes, so for them are tuned the harmonious eight sounds to stir their hearts; The mouth delights in the five flavors, so for it are prepared many delicacies, sour and salty, to bring out their excellence; The emotions delight in precious and good things, so for them are polished jade tablets to convey their intentions. Therefore the great carriage uses rush mats, the leather cap with cloth skirt, vermilion strings on resonant zithers, great broth and clear wine - these serve to prevent licentious extravagance and save from carved decay. Thus from the ruler and ministers in court with their sequence of honored and lowly, noble and base, down to the common people with their distinctions in carriages, clothing, palaces, food, marriages, funerals, and sacrifices - all matters have their appropriate adaptations, all things have their regulated patterns. Confucius said: 'As for the di sacrifice, from after the libation onward, I do not wish to observe it.'

When the Zhou declined, rites were abandoned and music ruined. Great and small overstepped each other. Guan Zhong's household possessed all three returns. Those who follow the law and maintain uprightness are insulted by the world, while those who are extravagant, presumptuous, and overstepping are called glorious. Even Zi Xia, one of the disciples' most distinguished students, still said: 'Going out I see variegated splendor and flourishing beauty that please me; entering I hear the Master's Dao and am happy. These two battle in my heart, and I cannot decide between them.' How much more so for those of middling and lower capacity, gradually immersed in failed teachings and clothed in established customs? Confucius said, 'It must be rectifying names,' but in Wei his position was not in harmony. After Confucius passed away, his disciples who received his teachings sank into obscurity and were not promoted. Some went to Qi and Chu, others disappeared into distant regions. How painful this is!

When Qin possessed the realm, it took in all the rites and ceremonies of the six states, selecting what was good from them. Though they did not match the sage's system, they honored the ruler and suppressed ministers, making the court flourish in proper order, following ancient ways. As for Emperor Gaozu, when he illuminated and possessed the four seas, Shusun Tong made considerable additions and subtractions, but generally all followed Qin's old ways. From the Son of Heaven's titles down to assistants and the names of palace chambers and officials, little was changed. When Emperor Wen ascended the throne, officials discussed wanting to establish ceremonial rites. Emperor Wen was fond of Daoist learning and considered that elaborate rites and decorated appearances were of no benefit to governance - what mattered was personal transformation. Therefore he dismissed them. In Emperor Jing's time, Imperial Secretary Chao Cuo was clear about worldly affairs and penal law. He earnestly admonished Emperor Jing several times saying: 'Feudal lords serve as screens and auxiliaries, ministers and sons follow one precedent. This is the system of ancient and present times.' 'Now the great states monopolize governance with different administrations and do not report to the capital. I fear this cannot be transmitted to posterity.' Emperor Jing used his plan, but then the six states rebelled. With Chao Cuo named first, the Son of Heaven executed Chao Cuo to resolve the difficulty. This matter is in Yuan Ang's biography. After this, officials only nourished connections and secured their stipends. No one dared discuss it again.

When the present Majesty ascended the throne, he summoned scholars of Confucian arts and ordered them to establish ceremonies together, but after more than ten years they had not completed it. Some said that in ancient times during great peace, when the ten thousand people were harmonious and happy, auspicious responses clearly arrived. Only then did they collect customs and establish creations. Rituals arise from human beings. Human life has desires. When desires are not satisfied, resentment inevitably arises. When resentment lacks proper measure, contention follows. Contention leads to chaos. The former kings hated this chaos, so they established rituals and righteousness to nourish people's desires and satisfy their needs, ensuring that desires do not exhaust the things available, nor things bend to desires. The two exist in mutual dependence and grow together - this is the origin of ritual. Therefore ritual is about nourishment. Rice, millet, and the five flavors - these nourish the mouth; Pepper, orchid, fragrant herbs, and chai - these nourish the nose; Bells, drums, pipes, and strings - these nourish the ears; Carved and engraved patterns and writings - these nourish the eyes; Spacious rooms, beds, mats, tables, and cushions - these nourish the body. Therefore ritual is about nourishment.
