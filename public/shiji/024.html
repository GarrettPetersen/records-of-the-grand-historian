Sima Qian remarks: The enlightened kings of high antiquity promoted music not to entertain themselves or indulge their desires, but to establish good governance. All proper education begins with music; when the music is correct, conduct follows suit. Thus music serves to stimulate the vital energies, circulate spiritual forces, and harmonize and correct the mind. Thus the gong mode stimulates the spleen to harmonize wisdom; shang stimulates the lungs to harmonize righteousness; jiao stimulates the liver to harmonize benevolence; zhi stimulates the heart to harmonize ritual propriety; yu stimulates the kidneys to harmonize wisdom. Thus music internally cultivates the proper mind while externally distinguishing social ranks. Above it serves the ancestral temples; below it transforms and elevates the common people. The zither measures eight chi and one cunâ€”the proper dimensions. The thickest string represents the gong mode and occupies the center, symbolizing the ruler. The shang string stretches to the right; the others follow in graded sizes without losing their proper orderâ€”thus the positions of ruler and ministers are correctly established. Thus hearing the gong mode makes one feel warm, relaxed, and expansive. Hearing the shang mode makes one principled and devoted to righteousness. Hearing the jiao mode arouses compassion and love for others. Hearing the zhi mode delights in virtue and encourages generosity. Hearing the yu mode promotes discipline and devotion to ritual. Ritual comes from external forms; music springs from inner feelings. Thus the noble person must never be separated from ritual, not even for a moment. A moment without ritual and violent, insolent behavior manifests externally. Nor can they be separated from music for a moment. A moment without music and corrupt, deviant impulses arise within. Thus music is the noble person's means of cultivating moral integrity. In ancient times, emperors and lords listened to bells and chimes without ever leaving the court; ministers listened to zithers and lutes without leaving their stations. This nurtured righteous conduct and prevented licentious excess. Licentious dissipation arises from lack of ritual. Thus sage kings ensured people heard the refined music of the Odes, saw the majestic forms of ceremony, performed respectful deportment, and spoke the principles of benevolence and righteousness. Thus the noble person, even while speaking all day, remains impervious to wicked and deviant influences.
