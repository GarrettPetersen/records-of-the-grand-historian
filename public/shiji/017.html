Sima Qian said: The Yin and earlier periods are still remote. The Zhou enfeoffed five ranks of nobility: dukes, marquises, earls, viscounts, and barons. Yet they enfeoffed Bo Qin and Kang Shu at Lu and Wei, each with four hundred li of territory, demonstrating the principle of favoring relatives and rewarding those with virtue; Tai Gong at Qi was given land combining five marquises' territories, honoring his diligent service. Kings Wu, Cheng, and Kang enfeoffed several hundred nobles, with fifty-five being paternal relatives, their territories ranging from no more than one hundred li down to thirty li, to support and protect the royal house. Guan, Cai, Kang Shu, Cao, and Zheng - some were given more territory, others less. After the reigns of Kings Li and You, the royal house declined, and powerful states ruled by marquises and earls arose. The Son of Heaven grew weak and could no longer control them. It was not that their virtue was impure, but that their position was weak.

When the Han arose, they established two ranks of nobility. In the later years of Gaozu's reign, anyone not of the Liu clan who became king, or who became a marquis without merit and without imperial approval, would be executed by all under heaven. Gaozu's sons and younger brothers of the same surname who became kings numbered nine states, and though only Changsha was of a different surname, there were more than a hundred meritorious ministers who became marquises. From Yanmen and Taiyuan eastward to Liaoyang became the Yan and Dai states; South of Mount Chang, where the Great Wall turns left, crossing the Yellow River and Ji Rivers, from E and Zhen eastward to the sea became the Qi and Zhao states; From Chen westward, south to the Nine Doubts Mountains, east along the Jiang, Huai, Gu, and Si Rivers, bordering Kuaiji, became the Liang, Chu, Huainan, and Changsha states: all bordering externally on the Hu and Yue barbarians. As for the inner lands north of the mountains eastward, all were the territories of feudal lords. The largest domains encompassed five or six commanderies, with dozens of connected cities. They established hundreds of officials and built palaces and observatories that rivaled the Son of Heaven. The Han alone controlled Sanhe, Dongjun, Yingchuan, and Nanyang commanderies, stretching from Jiangling westward to Shu and northward from Yunzhong to Longxi. Together with the capital region, this made fifteen commanderies in total. Many princesses and marquises also held fiefs within these territories. Why was this so? When the realm was first pacified, there were few close relatives of the same surname, so the emperor strengthened collateral branches of the family to maintain control over the four seas and support the Son of Heaven.

After a century of Han rule, family ties grew distant. Some feudal lords became arrogant and extravagant, swayed by slandering ministers who schemed for chaos. The greater lords rebelled, the lesser ones broke the law, endangering their lives and leading to their destruction and the loss of their states. The Son of Heaven, observing ancient precedents, then showed favor by allowing feudal lords to extend grace to their younger sons and divide their territories among them. Thus Qi was divided into seven parts, Zhao into six, Liang into five, and Huainan into three. Even the Son of Heaven's collateral sons became kings, and kings' collateral sons became marquises - more than a hundred in total. During the Wu and Chu rebellions, successive feudal lords lost territories due to their transgressions. Thus Yan and Dai had no northern border commanderies, while Wu, Huainan, and Changsha had no southern border commanderies. The branch commanderies of Qi, Zhao, Liang, and Chu - with their famous mountains, dikes, and coastal regions - all reverted to Han control. The power of the feudal lords was gradually diminished. The greatest states controlled no more than ten or so cities, while the smallest marquises held no more than several dozen li of territory. This was enough to fulfill their tribute obligations above and maintain sacrificial rites below, while guarding and supporting the capital. The Han controlled eighty to ninety commanderies, interspersed among the feudal territories like interlocking dog teeth. They controlled the strategic passes and terrain advantages, strengthening the trunk while weakening the branches and leaves. Thus the hierarchy was clear and everything fell into its proper place.
