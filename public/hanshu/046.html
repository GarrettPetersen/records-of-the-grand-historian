1
季布，楚人也，為任俠有名。 項籍使將兵，數窘漢王。 項籍滅，高祖購求布千金，敢有舍匿，罪三族。 布匿濮陽周氏，周氏曰：「漢求將軍急，跡且至臣家，能聽臣，臣敢進計； 即否，願先自剄。」 布許之。 乃髡鉗布，衣褐，置廣柳車中，并與其家僮數十人，之魯朱家所賣之。 朱家心知其季布也，買置田舍。 乃之雒陽見汝陰侯滕公，說曰：「季布何罪？ 臣各為其主用，職耳。 項氏臣豈可盡誅邪？ 今上始得天下，而以私怨求一人，何示不廣也！ 且以季布之賢，漢求之急如此，此不北走胡，南走越耳。 夫忌壯士以資敵國，此伍子胥所以鞭荊平之墓也。 君何不從容為上言之？」 滕公心知朱家大俠，意布匿其所，乃許諾。 侍間，果言如朱家指。 上乃赦布。 當是時，諸公皆多布能摧剛為柔，朱家亦以此名聞當世。 布召見，謝，拜郎中。
Ji Bu was from Chu and had made a name for himself as a knight-errant who took up others' causes. Xiang Yu put him in command of troops, and time and again he cornered the King of Han. After Xiang Yu fell, the High Emperor posted a reward of a thousand pounds of gold for Ji Bu, with the threat that anyone who hid him would suffer the punishment of guilt extending to three degrees of kinship. Ji Bu took refuge with the Zhou family of Puyang. They told him, 'The court is hunting you relentlessly; their agents will trace you here before long. If you will listen to me, I have a plan to propose;' If you will not, I would sooner cut my own throat first.' Ji Bu agreed. They shaved his head, fitted him with the iron collar, dressed him in a beggar's russet, stowed him in a funeral wagon, and, with several dozen of his own household slaves in tow, drove him to the Zhu household in Lu to sell him off as a slave. Zhu Jia recognized him at once as Ji Bu, purchased him, and lodged him on one of his farms. He then traveled to Luoyang to see the Marquis of Yiyin, Lord Teng, and argued, 'What wrong has Ji Bu done?' Each minister served his own master; that was nothing more than doing his duty. You cannot execute every minister who once served the Xiang house. The Son of Heaven has only just won the realm, yet he would hunt one man for a private slight—what kind of largeness of mind does that display? A man of Ji Bu's caliber, hounded this fiercely, will flee north to the Xiongnu or south to Yue—that is all you will achieve. Spurning a formidable warrior only arms a rival power; it was for that reason Wu Zixu lashed the grave of King Ping of Chu. Why not find a quiet moment and put this to the emperor yourself?' Lord Teng knew Zhu Jia for a famous knight-errant and guessed Ji Bu lay in his care, so he gave his word. When he next had the emperor's ear alone, he repeated Zhu Jia's argument point for point. The emperor then pardoned Ji Bu. Men of rank admired Ji Bu for turning disaster into survival, and Zhu Jia's name rang through the realm for the same deed. Ji Bu was called to court, offered thanks, and appointed gentleman of the palace.
2
孝惠時，為中郎將。 單于嘗為書嫚呂太后，太后怒，召諸將議之。 上將軍樊噲曰：「臣願得十萬眾，橫行匈奴中。」 諸將皆阿呂太后，以噲言為然。 布曰：「樊噲可斬也。 夫以高帝兵三十餘萬，困於平城，噲時亦在其中。 今噲奈何以十萬眾橫行匈奴中，面謾！ 且秦以事胡，陳勝等起。 今瘡痍未瘳，噲又面諛，欲搖動天下。」 是時殿上皆恐，太后罷朝，遂不復議擊匈奴事。
Under Emperor Xiaohui he served as general of the gentlemen of the palace. The Chanyu had once sent a letter that grossly insulted Empress Dowager Lü. Enraged, she called the generals in council. Senior general Fan Kuai declared, 'Give me a hundred thousand troops and I will sweep through the heart of the Xiongnu lands.' The generals to a man curried favor with the empress dowager and agreed with Fan Kuai. Ji Bu said, 'Fan Kuai deserves execution. The High Emperor took more than three hundred thousand men to Pingcheng and was surrounded there—and Fan Kuai was in that army. How can the same man now promise to ride roughshod over the Xiongnu with a mere hundred thousand? That is bare-faced bravado.' Besides, the Qin court exhausted the realm on the northern frontier, and Chen Sheng and his like rose in rebellion. The wounds of war have barely closed, and Fan Kuai would flatter you to your face and risk unsettling the whole realm again.' The courtiers in the hall were terrified. The empress dowager ended the session, and the plan to attack the Xiongnu was dropped.
3
布為河東守。 孝文時，人有言其賢，召欲以為御史大夫。 人又言其勇，使酒難近。 至，留邸一月，見罷。 布進曰：「臣待罪河東，陛下無故召臣，此人必有以臣欺陛下者。 今臣至，無所受事，罷去，此人必有毀臣者。 夫陛下以一人譽召臣，一人毀去臣，臣恐天下有識者聞之，有以窺陛下。」 上默然，慚曰：「河東吾股肱郡，故特召君耳。」 布之官。
Ji Bu was appointed governor of Hedong. Under Emperor Xiaowen, someone praised his talent, and he was summoned to the capital with the idea of appointing him imperial counselor. Others then spoke of his fierceness—that he was quarrelsome in his cups and difficult to work with. He reached the capital and waited a full month in the official lodge before being sent away without an audience. Ji Bu stepped forward and said, 'I bore my office at Hedong in fear of fault; Your Majesty summoned me without cause—someone must have oversold me to you. Now I have come, been given no charge, and am dismissed—someone must have run me down behind my back. If a single voice of praise can bring me to court and a single whisper of blame can send me packing, I fear thoughtful men everywhere will read something into Your Majesty's judgment.' The emperor fell silent, then said with embarrassment, 'Hedong is one of the sinews of the realm; I called you for that reason alone.' Ji Bu returned to his duties in Hedong.
4
辯士曹丘生數招權顧金錢，事貴人趙談等，與竇長君善。 布聞，寄書諫長君曰：「吾聞曹丘生非長者，勿與通。」 及曹丘生歸，欲得書請布。 竇長君曰：「季將軍不說足下，足下無往。」 固請書，遂行。 使人先發書，布果大怒，待曹丘。 曹丘至，則揖布曰：「楚人諺曰『得黃金百，不如得季布諾』，足下何以得此聲梁楚之間哉？ 且僕與足下俱楚人，使僕游揚足下名於天下，顧不美乎？ 何足下距僕之深也！」 布乃大說。 引入，留數月，為上客，厚送之。 布名所以益聞者，曹丘揚之也。
The persuader Cao Qiusheng traded on influence for gold, curried favor with men such as the powerful Zhao Tan, and was close to Lord Dou the elder. Ji Bu heard of it and wrote to Lord Dou warning him, 'Cao Qiusheng is no gentleman; have nothing to do with him.' When Cao Qiusheng came home, he wanted a letter of introduction so he could call on Ji Bu. Lord Dou replied, 'General Ji dislikes you; you had better not go.' Cao pressed until he got the letter and set out. He sent a messenger ahead with the letter; Ji Bu flew into a rage and waited for him. When Cao Qiusheng arrived, he bowed and said, 'The people of Chu say, "A hundred pounds of gold cannot match one promise from Ji Bu"—how did you earn such fame across Liang and Chu?' We are both sons of Chu; if I trumpeted your name to the world, would that not be splendid?' Why do you shut me out so coldly!' Ji Bu was delighted. He brought him inside, kept him for months as an honored guest, and sent him on his way with rich gifts. It was Cao Qiusheng's praise that made Ji Bu's name resound even farther.
5
布弟季心氣蓋關中，遇人恭謹，為任俠，方數千里，士爭為死。 嘗殺人，亡吳，從爰絲匿，長事爰絲，弟畜灌夫、籍福之屬。 嘗為中司馬，中尉郅都不敢加。 少年多時時竊借其名以行。 當是時，季心以勇，布以諾，聞關中。
Ji Bu's younger brother Ji Xin dominated Guanzhong in sheer force of character, yet treated others with deference; as a knight-errant his fame ran a thousand miles, and fighting men competed for the honor of dying at his side. He had once killed a man and fled to Wu, where he took refuge with Yuan Ang, serving him as an elder patron while treating Guan Fu, Ji Fu, and their set as juniors. He served as marshal of the center; even Commandant Zhi Du of the capital did not dare cross him. Young blades habitually invoked his name without permission. In those days Ji Xin was famed for valor and Ji Bu for keeping his word throughout Guanzhong.
6
布母弟丁公，為項羽將，逐窘高祖彭城西。 短兵接，漢王急，顧謂丁公曰：「兩賢豈相厄哉！」 丁公引兵而還。 及項王滅，丁公謁見高祖，以丁公徇軍中，曰：「丁公為項王臣不忠，使項王失天下者也。」 遂斬之，曰：「使後為人臣無傚丁公也！」
Ji Bu's maternal half-brother Ding Gong was a general under Xiang Yu who harried the High Emperor west of Pengcheng. In close combat the King of Han was hard pressed, turned to Ding Gong, and cried, 'Two good men do not corner each other!' Ding Gong called off his troops and drew back. After Xiang Yu fell, Ding Gong presented himself to the High Emperor, who paraded him before the host and declared, 'Ding Gong served King Xiang without loyalty; he is the sort of man who cost his master the empire.' He had him beheaded, adding, 'Let no future minister take Ding Gong as his model!'
7
欒布，梁人也。 彭越為家人時，嘗與布游，窮困，賣庸於齊，為酒家保。 數歲別去，而布為人所略，賣為奴於燕。 為其主家報仇，燕將臧荼舉以為都尉。 荼為燕王，布為將。 及荼反，漢擊燕，虜布。 梁王彭越聞之，乃言上，請贖布為梁大夫。 使於齊，未反，漢召彭越責以謀反，夷三族，梟首雒陽，下詔有收視者輒捕之。 布還，奏事彭越頭下，祠而哭之。 吏捕以聞。 上召布罵曰：「若與彭越反邪？ 吾禁人勿收，若獨祠而哭之，與反明矣。 趣亨之。」 方提趨湯，顧曰：「願一言而死。」 上曰：「何言？」 布曰：「方上之困彭城，敗滎陽、成皋間，項王所以不能遂西，徒以彭王居梁地，與漢合從苦楚也。 當是之時，彭王壹顧，與楚則漢破，與漢則楚破。 且垓下之會，微彭王，項氏不亡。 天下已定，彭王剖符受封，亦欲傳之萬世。 今漢壹徵兵於梁，彭王病不行，而疑以為反。 反形未見，以苛細誅之，臣恐功臣人人自危也。 今彭王已死，臣生不如死，請就亨。」 上乃釋布，拜為都尉。
Luan Bu was a native of Liang. While Peng Yue was still a commoner, he and Luan Bu had traveled together; destitute, they hired out as laborers in Qi and Luan Bu tended tables in an inn. Years later they went separate ways, and Luan Bu was seized and sold into slavery in Yan. He avenged his master's family; the Yan general Zang Tu singled him out and appointed him commandant. When Zang Tu became king of Yan, Luan Bu served as his general. When Zang Tu rose in rebellion, the Han forces attacked Yan and took Luan Bu prisoner. King Peng Yue of Liang petitioned the throne to ransom him and appoint him a grandee of Liang. Luan Bu was on a mission to Qi and had not returned when the court summoned Peng Yue, condemned him for treason, wiped out three degrees of his kin, exposed his head at Luoyang, and decreed that anyone who dared mourn or view the remains would be seized. Luan Bu came back, made his report beneath Peng Yue's severed head, offered sacrifice, and wept. Officials seized him and informed the throne. The emperor had Luan Bu dragged in and shouted, 'Did you conspire with Peng Yue?' I forbade anyone to mourn him; you alone held rites and wept—that is open rebellion.' Boil him at once.' As they hauled him toward the boiling cauldron, he turned and said, 'Let me speak one sentence before I die.' The emperor asked, 'What is it?' Luan Bu said, 'When you were pinned down at Pengcheng and driven back between Xingyang and Chenggao, the only reason Xiang Yu could not push west was that King Peng held the Liang lands in alliance with Han and tormented Chu from the rear. At that moment a single shift of Peng Yue's loyalty would have shattered Han if he joined Chu, or shattered Chu if he stayed with Han. At Gaixia, without Peng Yue, the house of Xiang would not have fallen. Peace won, King Peng received his bronze tally and fief, intending them to pass down for generations. Now a single call-up from Liang found him too ill to march, and you branded him a traitor. No act of rebellion had appeared; to kill him on trifling suspicion will make every accomplished minister wonder who is next. Peng Yue is dead; for me to live would be worse than death. Send me to the cauldron.' The emperor spared him and appointed him commandant.
8
孝文時，為燕相，至將軍。 布稱曰：「窮困不能辱身，非人也； 富貴不能快意，非賢也。」 於是嘗有德，厚報之； 有怨，必以法滅之。 吳楚反時，以功封為鄃侯，復為燕相。 燕齊之間皆為立社，號曰欒公社。
Under Emperor Xiaowen he rose from chancellor of Yan to general. Luan Bu used to say, 'To be poor and yet refuse to demean yourself is less than human;' To be rich and honored and yet unable to satisfy your desires is less than worthy.' Those who had done him a kindness he repaid lavishly; Anyone who had earned his enmity he destroyed by legal process. During the revolt of Wu and Chu he earned the title Marquis of Yu and returned as chancellor of Yan. From Yan to Qi the people set up shrines to him, calling them the altars of Lord Luan.
9
布薨，子賁嗣侯，孝武時坐為太常犧牲不如令，國除。
Luan Bu died and his son Luan Ben inherited the marquisate; under Emperor Wu he lost the fief for letting sacrificial animals fall short of ritual standards while serving as grand master of ceremonies.
10
田叔，趙陘城人也。 其先，齊田氏也。 叔好劍，學黃老術於樂鉅公。 為人廉直，喜任俠。 游諸公，趙人舉之趙相趙午，言之趙王張敖，以為郎中。 數歲，趙王賢之，未及遷。
Tian Shu came from Xingcheng in Zhao. His forebears were the Tian lineage of Qi. He loved fencing and studied Huang-Lao doctrine under Yue Ju. He was honest, upright, and drawn to the knight-errant's code. He moved in elite circles until the people of Zhao recommended him to Chancellor Zhao Wu, who mentioned him to King Zhang Ao of Zhao, who appointed him gentleman of the palace. For several years the king admired him but had not yet promoted him.
11
會趙午、貫高等謀弒上，事發覺，漢下詔捕趙王及群臣反者。 趙有敢隨王，罪三族。 唯田叔、孟舒等十餘人赭衣自髡鉗，隨王至長安。 趙王敖事白，得出，廢王為宣平侯，乃進言叔等十人。 上召見，與語，漢廷臣無能出其右者。 上說，盡拜為郡守、諸侯相。 叔為漢中守十餘年。
Then Zhao Wu, Guan Gao, and their faction plotted to kill the emperor; the plot was uncovered, and Han issued warrants for King Zhang Ao of Zhao and every minister implicated in treason. In Zhao anyone who dared stand by the king would share guilt to the third degree of kinship. Only Tian Shu, Meng Shu, and a dozen companions donned convict russet, shaved their own heads, and clamped on the iron collar to escort their king to Chang'an. When Zhang Ao's name was cleared, he left prison, was stripped of his kingship and enfeoffed as Marquis of Xuanping, and then recommended Tian Shu and his nine companions to the throne. The emperor received them and spoke with each in turn; not one minister at court could match their poise. Pleased with them all, he named every man either a commandery governor or a feudal chancellor. Tian Shu governed Hanzhong for well over a decade.
12
孝文帝初立，召叔問曰：「公知天下長者乎？」 對曰：「臣何足以知之！」 上曰：「公長者，宜知之。」 叔頓首曰：「故雲中守孟舒，長者也。」 是時孟舒坐虜大入雲中免。 上曰：「先帝置孟舒雲中十餘年矣，虜常一入，孟舒不能堅守，無故士卒戰死者數百人。 長者固殺人乎？」 叔叩頭曰：「夫貫高等謀反，天子下明詔，趙有敢隨張王者罪三族，然孟舒自髡鉗，隨張王，以身死之，豈自知為雲中守哉！ 漢與楚相距，士卒罷敝，而匈奴冒頓新服北夷，來為邊寇，孟舒知士卒罷敝，不忍出言，士爭臨城死敵，如子為父，以故死者數百人，孟舒豈敺之哉！ 是乃孟舒所以為長者。」 於是上曰：「賢哉孟舒！」 復召以為雲中守。
Early in Emperor Xiaowen's reign he called Tian Shu in and asked, 'Who in the empire commands respect as a true elder statesman?' Tian Shu answered, 'How could a man like me presume to judge such things?' The emperor said, 'You yourself are such a man; you must have an opinion.' Tian Shu kowtowed and said, 'The former governor of Yunzhong, Meng Shu, is the one I would name.' Meng Shu had just been stripped of office for letting a large Xiongnu raid penetrate Yunzhong. The emperor objected: 'The late emperor kept Meng Shu at Yunzhong for years; the Xiongnu broke in once in strength, he failed to hold the line, and hundreds of men died without cause. Is that what you call the conduct of a humane elder?' Do worthy men make a habit of sending men to their deaths?' Tian Shu kowtowed and replied, 'When Guan Gao conspired treason, the court decreed that anyone in Zhao who stayed with King Zhang Ao would die with three degrees of kin—yet Meng Shu mutilated himself like a convict and followed his lord into captivity, prepared to die. He could not then have dreamed he would one day govern Yunzhong.' Han and Chu were locked in stalemate and the army was spent; Modu had just cowed the northern peoples and turned south to harry the frontier. Meng Shu saw how broken his men were and could not bring himself to drive them out; the soldiers themselves rushed to the walls and died like sons defending a father—hence the heavy losses. Meng Shu did not hound them to that fate.' That is precisely what makes Meng Shu a true elder.' The emperor exclaimed, 'Meng Shu is a remarkable man!' He recalled Meng Shu and restored him as governor of Yunzhong.
13
後數歲，叔坐法失官。 梁孝王使人殺漢議臣爰盎，景帝召叔案梁，具得其事。 還報，上曰：「梁有之乎？」 對曰：「有之。」 「事安在？」 叔曰：「上無以梁事為問也。 今梁王不伏誅，是廢漢法也； 如其伏誅，太后食不甘味，臥不安席，此憂在陛下。」 於是上大賢之，以為魯相。
Some years afterward Tian Shu lost his post over a legal infraction. When Prince Xiao of Liang had the Han adviser Yuan Ang murdered, Emperor Jing sent Tian Shu to probe the kingdom of Liang, and he extracted the whole truth. On his return the emperor asked him bluntly, 'Did Liang do it?' Tian Shu answered, 'Yes.' 'What proof do you have?' Tian Shu said, 'Your Majesty should not pursue this question about Liang.' If the Prince of Liang is not punished, you cast aside the law of Han. If you put him to death, the empress dowager will neither eat nor sleep at ease, and that sorrow will fall on Your Majesty alone.' The emperor admired his tact and appointed him chancellor to the king of Lu.
14
相初至官，民以王取其財物自言者百餘人。 叔取其渠率二千人笞，怒之曰：「王非汝主邪？ 何敢自言主！」 魯王聞之，大慚，發中府錢，使相償之。 相曰：「王自使人償之，不爾，是王為惡而相為善也。」
Soon after he took office, more than a hundred people came forward on their own, accusing the king of seizing their property. Tian Shu had the ringleaders—some two thousand men—rounded up and flogged, shouting, 'The king is your sovereign; how dare you denounce him in open court!' Who gave you leave to accuse your master?' The king of Lu was mortified, opened the palace treasury, and told the chancellor to pay the claimants. Tian Shu refused: 'Send your own stewards to make amends; otherwise it will look as though you committed the wrong and I bought cheap virtue.'
15
魯王好獵，相常從入苑中，王輒休相就館。 相常暴坐苑外，終不休，曰：「吾王暴露，獨何為舍？」 王以故不大出遊。
The king of Lu loved the chase; Tian Shu always rode with him into the hunting park, and the king would dismiss him to the guesthouse to rest. Tian Shu instead sat in the open outside the preserve and refused to leave, saying, 'If my king endures the weather, how can I hide indoors?' Shamed into it, the king gave up most of his long hunting trips.
16
數年以官卒，魯以百金祠，少子仁不受，曰：「義不傷先人名。」
He died in office a few years later. Lu offered a hundred pounds of gold for his rites, but his youngest son Tian Ren declined, saying, 'Right feeling forbids me to trade on my father's reputation.'
17
仁以壯勇為衛將軍舍人，數從擊匈奴。 衛將軍進言仁為郎中，至二千石、丞相長史，失官。 後使刺三河，還，奏事稱意，拜為京輔都尉。 月餘，遷司直。 數歲，戾太子舉兵，仁部閉城門，令太子得亡，坐縱反者族。
Tian Ren, bold and hardy, served as a household retainer to General Wei Qing and rode with him on many campaigns against the Xiongnu. Wei Qing recommended him for gentleman of the palace; he rose to two thousand shi and chief clerk under the chancellor, then lost his post. Later he was sent as imperial censor to the Three He circuit; his memorial on returning pleased the emperor, and he was appointed metropolitan commandant of the capital region. A little over a month later he was promoted to imperial investigator. Years later, when Heir Apparent Liu Ju rose in arms, Tian Ren, commanding his detachment, had the city gates shut in a manner that allowed the prince to slip away; he was convicted of releasing a traitor and his whole clan was put to death.
18
贊曰：以項羽之氣，而季布以勇顯名楚，身履軍搴旗者數矣，可謂壯士。 及至困厄奴僇，苟活而不變，何也？ 彼自負其材，受辱不羞，欲有所用其未足也，故終為漢名將。 賢者誠重其死。 夫婢妾賤人，感概而自殺，非能勇也，其畫無俚之至耳。 欒布哭彭越，田叔隨張敖，赴死如歸，彼誠知所處，雖古烈士，何以加哉！
The historian's judgment: For all of Xiang Yu's towering rage, Ji Bu won fame in Chu through sheer nerve, time and again charging the line and snatching enemy standards—a true fighting man. Yet when ruin drove him to the disgrace of slavery, he endured and did not break—why was that? They trusted their own ability; they could bear shame because they still had more to give the world, and so they finished as celebrated generals of Han. Men of true worth do not throw their lives away cheaply. Maids, concubines, and common folk who take offense and fall on their blades are not brave; their outlook is hopelessly mean and narrow. Luan Bu mourned Peng Yue at the risk of his life; Tian Shu shackled himself to Zhang Ao and marched toward execution as calmly as a man going home. They knew exactly where they stood; the steadfast worthies of old could scarcely have outdone them.
