Volume 37: Ji Bu, Luan Bu and Tian Shu

1
季布，楚人也，為任俠有名。 項籍使將兵，數窘漢王。 項籍滅，高祖購求布千金，敢有舍匿，罪三族。 布匿濮陽周氏，周氏曰：「漢求將軍急，跡且至臣家，能聽臣，臣敢進計； 即否，願先自剄。」 布許之。 乃髡鉗布，衣褐，置廣柳車中，并與其家僮數十人，之魯朱家所賣之。 朱家心知其季布也，買置田舍。 乃之雒陽見汝陰侯滕公，說曰：「季布何罪？ 臣各為其主用，職耳。 項氏臣豈可盡誅邪？ 今上始得天下，而以私怨求一人，何示不廣也！ 且以季布之賢，漢求之急如此，此不北走胡，南走越耳。 夫忌壯士以資敵國，此伍子胥所以鞭荊平之墓也。 君何不從容為上言之？」 滕公心知朱家大俠，意布匿其所，乃許諾。 侍間，果言如朱家指。 上乃赦布。 當是時，諸公皆多布能摧剛為柔，朱家亦以此名聞當世。 布召見，謝，拜郎中。
Ji Bu was from Chu and had won renown as a knight-errant who stood by his sworn obligations. Xiang Yu put him in command of troops, and time and again he cornered the King of Han. After Xiang Yu fell, Gaozu posted a bounty of a thousand pounds of gold for Ji Bu, with death for the offender’s whole extended family if anyone sheltered him. Ji Bu took refuge with the Zhou family of Puyang. The head of the family told him, “The court is hunting you relentlessly, and their agents will soon be at my door. If you will trust me, I have a plan; if not, I would rather slit my own throat first.” Ji Bu agreed. They then shaved his head and collared him like a convict, dressed him in a poor man’s cloak, hid him in a rough transport wagon along with dozens of family retainers, and sold the whole lot to Zhu Jia of Lu. Zhu Jia recognized him at once as Ji Bu, bought him, and installed him on his estate. He then traveled to Luoyang to see Xiahou Ying, the Marquis of Ruoyin, and argued, “What wrong has Ji Bu done? Each minister served his own lord—that was simply his duty. You cannot execute every last minister who once served the house of Xiang. The Son of Heaven has only just won the realm; to hound one man out of personal spite shows a remarkably small mind. Besides, Ji Bu is a man of real ability; push him this hard and he will end up in the Xiongnu’s camp to the north or Nanyue to the south. Driving a formidable warrior into the enemy’s arms is how you get another King Ping of Chu and another Wu Zixu desecrating royal tombs. Why not find a quiet moment and put this to His Majesty yourself?” Xiahou Ying knew Zhu Jia’s reputation as a great knight-errant and guessed Ji Bu lay hidden on his lands; he agreed to speak. When he next attended the emperor, he made the case exactly as Zhu Jia had outlined. The emperor then pardoned Ji Bu. At the time, leading figures admired Ji Bu for learning to bend when he could not break, and Zhu Jia’s name rang through the age for it. Ji Bu was summoned to audience, thanked the throne for his life, and was appointed a gentleman of the palace.
2
孝惠時，為中郎將。 單于嘗為書嫚呂太后，太后怒，召諸將議之。 上將軍樊噲曰：「臣願得十萬眾，橫行匈奴中。」 諸將皆阿呂太后，以噲言為然。 布曰：「樊噲可斬也。 夫以高帝兵三十餘萬，困於平城，噲時亦在其中。 今噲奈何以十萬眾橫行匈奴中，面謾！ 且秦以事胡，陳勝等起。 今瘡痍未瘳，噲又面諛，欲搖動天下。」 是時殿上皆恐，太后罷朝，遂不復議擊匈奴事。
Under Emperor Hui he served as general of the gentlemen of the palace. The Chanyu had once sent a letter that insulted Empress Dowager Lü; enraged, she called in her generals to discuss a response. Fan Kuai, the senior general, declared, “Give me a hundred thousand men and I will drive straight through the heart of the Xiongnu steppe.” The other generals curried favor with the empress dowager and echoed Fan Kuai’s bravado. Ji Bu said, “Fan Kuai deserves execution. When Gaodi’s three hundred thousand troops were trapped at Pingcheng, Fan Kuai was right there in the ranks. How dare he now boast that a mere hundred thousand could overrun the Xiongnu heartland? He is lying to your faces! Remember that the Qin empire cracked in rebellion when it threw everything into war on the northern frontier. The realm is still bleeding from those wounds, and Fan Kuai flatters you to your faces and would set the world trembling again.” The courtiers on the dais were stricken with fear; the empress dowager cut short the session and never again brought up striking the Xiongnu.
3
布為河東守。 孝文時，人有言其賢，召欲以為御史大夫。 人又言其勇，使酒難近。 至，留邸一月，見罷。 布進曰：「臣待罪河東，陛下無故召臣，此人必有以臣欺陛下者。 今臣至，無所受事，罷去，此人必有毀臣者。 夫陛下以一人譽召臣，一人毀去臣，臣恐天下有識者聞之，有以窺陛下。」 上默然，慚曰：「河東吾股肱郡，故特召君耳。」 布之官。
Ji Bu was appointed governor of Hedong. Under Emperor Wen someone praised his talent, and the court summoned him intending to name him imperial clerk. Others spoke of his fierceness and his bullying ways when drunk, warning that he was difficult to work with. When he reached the capital he was kept waiting a month in the official hostel and then sent home without the post. Ji Bu stepped forward and said, “I was serving at Hedong when Your Majesty summoned me without cause—someone must have oversold me to you. Now that I am here you give me no charge and send me away—someone has clearly blackened my name. If one man’s praise brings me to court and one man’s slander sends me packing, every thoughtful subject in the realm will know how easily Your Majesty can be swayed.” The emperor fell silent, then said with embarrassment, “Hedong is one of my vital provinces; I simply wanted a look at you.” Ji Bu returned to his post.
4
辯士曹丘生數招權顧金錢，事貴人趙談等，與竇長君善。 布聞，寄書諫長君曰：「吾聞曹丘生非長者，勿與通。」 及曹丘生歸，欲得書請布。 竇長君曰：「季將軍不說足下，足下無往。」 固請書，遂行。 使人先發書，布果大怒，待曹丘。 曹丘至，則揖布曰：「楚人諺曰『得黃金百，不如得季布諾』，足下何以得此聲梁楚之間哉？ 且僕與足下俱楚人，使僕游揚足下名於天下，顧不美乎？ 何足下距僕之深也！」 布乃大說。 引入，留數月，為上客，厚送之。 布名所以益聞者，曹丘揚之也。
The persuader Cao Qiusheng traded on connections for cash, flattered powerful men such as Zhao Tan, and cultivated Dou Changjun. When Ji Bu heard of it he wrote Dou Changjun a warning: “Cao Qiusheng is no gentleman—have nothing to do with him.” When Cao Qiusheng came home he wanted a letter of introduction so he could call on Ji Bu. Dou Changjun said, “General Ji dislikes you; you had better not go.” Cao pressed until Dou gave him a letter, then set out. He sent a messenger ahead with the letter; Ji Bu flew into a rage and waited for Cao Qiusheng. When Cao Qiusheng arrived he bowed and said, “Chu has a saying: ‘A hundred catties of gold cannot buy Ji Bu’s word’—how did you earn that reputation all the way from Liang to Chu? We are both sons of Chu; if I trumpet your name across the empire, would that not serve you well? Why do you shut me out so coldly!” Ji Bu was delighted. He brought him inside, kept him for months as an honored guest, and sent him away laden with gifts. It was Cao Qiusheng’s praise that made Ji Bu’s name ring louder still.
5
布弟季心氣蓋關中，遇人恭謹，為任俠，方數千里，士爭為死。 嘗殺人，亡吳，從爰絲匿，長事爰絲，弟畜灌夫、籍福之屬。 嘗為中司馬，中尉郅都不敢加。 少年多時時竊借其名以行。 當是時，季心以勇，布以諾，聞關中。
Ji Bu’s younger brother Ji Xin’s reputation overshadowed Guanzhong; though courteous to everyone he met, as a knight-errant his name drew willing followers for a thousand miles around. He had once killed a man and fled to Wu, where he hid with Yuan Ang, served him with the deference of a junior, and treated Guan Fu and Ji Fu as younger brothers. As marshal of the center he was a man even the dreaded Commandant Zhi Du would not provoke. Young ruffians constantly invoked his name to throw their weight around. At the time Guanzhong knew Ji Xin for daring and Ji Bu for keeping his word.
6
布母弟丁公，為項羽將，逐窘高祖彭城西。 短兵接，漢王急，顧謂丁公曰：「兩賢豈相厄哉！」 丁公引兵而還。 及項王滅，丁公謁見高祖，以丁公徇軍中，曰：「丁公為項王臣不忠，使項王失天下者也。」 遂斬之，曰：「使後為人臣無傚丁公也！」
Ji Bu’s half-brother on his mother’s side, Ding Gong, served Xiang Yu as a general and once chased Gaozu into a corner west of Pengcheng. At close quarters the King of Han was desperate; glancing back he cried to Ding Gong, “Would two good men destroy each other?” Ding Gong broke off the pursuit and withdrew his troops. After Xiang Yu fell, Ding Gong presented himself to Gaozu, who paraded him before the host and declared, “Here is a minister of Xiang Yu who broke faith—the man who cost his king the empire.” He then had him beheaded, adding, “Let no future minister take Ding Gong as his model!”
7
欒布，梁人也。 彭越為家人時，嘗與布游，窮困，賣庸於齊，為酒家保。 數歲別去，而布為人所略，賣為奴於燕。 為其主家報仇，燕將臧荼舉以為都尉。 荼為燕王，布為將。 及荼反，漢擊燕，虜布。 梁王彭越聞之，乃言上，請贖布為梁大夫。 使於齊，未反，漢召彭越責以謀反，夷三族，梟首雒陽，下詔有收視者輒捕之。 布還，奏事彭越頭下，祠而哭之。 吏捕以聞。 上召布罵曰：「若與彭越反邪？ 吾禁人勿收，若獨祠而哭之，與反明矣。 趣亨之。」 方提趨湯，顧曰：「願一言而死。」 上曰：「何言？」 布曰：「方上之困彭城，敗滎陽、成皋間，項王所以不能遂西，徒以彭王居梁地，與漢合從苦楚也。 當是之時，彭王壹顧，與楚則漢破，與漢則楚破。 且垓下之會，微彭王，項氏不亡。 天下已定，彭王剖符受封，亦欲傳之萬世。 今漢壹徵兵於梁，彭王病不行，而疑以為反。 反形未見，以苛細誅之，臣恐功臣人人自危也。 今彭王已死，臣生不如死，請就亨。」 上乃釋布，拜為都尉。
Luan Bu was from Liang. When Peng Yue was still a commoner he had traveled with Luan Bu; down and out, he hired himself out in Qi and worked as a potman in an inn. Years later they parted ways, and Luan Bu was kidnapped and sold into slavery in Yan. He avenged his master’s household, and the Yan general Zang Tu singled him out and appointed him commandant. When Zang Tu became king of Yan, Luan Bu served as his general. When Zang Tu rebelled, Han forces attacked Yan and took Luan Bu prisoner. King Peng Yue of Liang heard of it and petitioned the emperor to ransom him and appoint him a grandee of Liang. Luan Bu was on a mission to Qi when, before he could return, the court summoned Peng Yue, convicted him of treason, wiped out his kin to the third degree, spiked his head at Luoyang, and decreed death for anyone who so much as claimed the body. When Luan Bu came back he presented his report beneath Peng Yue’s severed head, offered sacrifice, and wept. Officials arrested him and reported it. The emperor had him brought in and snarled, “Were you in league with Peng Yue? I forbade anyone to mourn him, yet you alone offered sacrifice and wept—that proves your guilt.” Take him away and boil him alive.” As they dragged him toward the boiling cauldron he looked back and said, “Let me speak one sentence before I die.” The emperor asked, “What is it?” Luan Bu said, “When you were trapped at Pengcheng and driven from Xingyang to Chenggao, the only reason Xiang Yu never drove west was that Peng Yue held Liang in alliance with Han and pinned Chu’s forces. At that moment a single shift of Peng Yue’s loyalty would have shattered Han had he joined Chu, or Chu had he joined Han. At Gaixia, without Peng Yue, the house of Xiang would never have fallen. When peace came, Peng Yue received his fief and bronze tallies like any other king, expecting his line to endure for ages. Then Han called up every able man in Liang; Peng Yue was too ill to march, and on that slender ground you branded him a traitor. No act of rebellion had appeared, yet you cut him down on petty pretexts—I fear every man who helped you win the throne now sleeps uneasy. Peng Yue is dead; for me to live would be worse than death—send me to the cauldron.” The emperor then spared him and named him commandant.
8
孝文時，為燕相，至將軍。 布稱曰：「窮困不能辱身，非人也； 富貴不能快意，非賢也。」 於是嘗有德，厚報之； 有怨，必以法滅之。 吳楚反時，以功封為鄃侯，復為燕相。 燕齊之間皆為立社，號曰欒公社。
Under Emperor Wen he rose from chancellor of Yan to general. Luan Bu used to say, “A man who in hard times will not endure a little humiliation is no true man; a man who gains wealth and power yet cannot have his due satisfaction is no worthy man.” Accordingly he repaid every kindness lavishly; and every slight he settled through the courts. When Wu and Chu rose in revolt, his service won him the marquisate of Yu and a second term as Yan’s chancellor. From Yan to Qi the people set up shrines in his honor and called them the altars of Lord Luan.
9
布薨，子賁嗣侯，孝武時坐為太常犧牲不如令，國除。
When Luan Bu died his son Fen inherited the title; under Emperor Wu the fief was stripped after Fen, as grand master of ceremonies, let the ritual victims fall short of statute.
10
田叔，趙陘城人也。 其先，齊田氏也。 叔好劍，學黃老術於樂鉅公。 為人廉直，喜任俠。 游諸公，趙人舉之趙相趙午，言之趙王張敖，以為郎中。 數歲，趙王賢之，未及遷。
Tian Shu came from Xingcheng in Zhao. His forebears were the Tian lineage of Qi. He loved fencing and studied Huang–Lao doctrine under Master Yue Ju. He was honest, upright, and drawn to the code of the knight-errant. He moved among Zhao’s notables until the people recommended him to Chancellor Zhao Wu, who introduced him to King Zhang Ao and had him appointed a gentleman of the palace. The king came to admire him, though years passed without a promotion.
11
會趙午、貫高等謀弒上，事發覺，漢下詔捕趙王及群臣反者。 趙有敢隨王，罪三族。 唯田叔、孟舒等十餘人赭衣自髡鉗，隨王至長安。 趙王敖事白，得出，廢王為宣平侯，乃進言叔等十人。 上召見，與語，漢廷臣無能出其右者。 上說，盡拜為郡守、諸侯相。 叔為漢中守十餘年。
Then Zhao Wu, Guan Gao, and others plotted to kill the emperor; when the plot surfaced, Han issued warrants for King Zhang Ao and every minister implicated. Zhao was warned that anyone who followed their king would die with his whole extended family. Only Tian Shu, Meng Shu, and a dozen others shaved their heads, donned the felon’s collar and crimson robe, and followed their king into captivity at Chang’an. Zhang Ao was cleared of the plot, released from prison, demoted from king to Marquis of Xuanping, and then recommended Tian Shu and the other nine to the throne. The emperor called them in; after talking with them he found that none of his own ministers could match them. Pleased, he gave them all posts as commandery governors or as chancellors of princely kingdoms. Tian Shu governed Hanzhong for over a decade.
12
孝文帝初立，召叔問曰：「公知天下長者乎？」 對曰：「臣何足以知之！」 上曰：「公長者，宜知之。」 叔頓首曰：「故雲中守孟舒，長者也。」 是時孟舒坐虜大入雲中免。 上曰：「先帝置孟舒雲中十餘年矣，虜常一入，孟舒不能堅守，無故士卒戰死者數百人。 長者固殺人乎？」 叔叩頭曰：「夫貫高等謀反，天子下明詔，趙有敢隨張王者罪三族，然孟舒自髡鉗，隨張王，以身死之，豈自知為雲中守哉！ 漢與楚相距，士卒罷敝，而匈奴冒頓新服北夷，來為邊寇，孟舒知士卒罷敝，不忍出言，士爭臨城死敵，如子為父，以故死者數百人，孟舒豈敺之哉！ 是乃孟舒所以為長者。」 於是上曰：「賢哉孟舒！」 復召以為雲中守。
Early in Emperor Wen’s reign he summoned Tian Shu and asked, “Who, in your judgment, are the most honorable men in the empire?” Tian Shu answered, “I am far too humble to say.” The emperor said, “You are such a man yourself—you must have an opinion.” Tian Shu kowtowed and named Meng Shu, the former governor of Yunzhong. Meng Shu had just been stripped of office after a major barbarian raid on Yunzhong. Wen objected: “The late emperor kept Meng Shu in Yunzhong for years, yet the raiders broke in and he could not hold the line—hundreds of soldiers died, and for what?” Is that how a “honorable man” behaves—by getting hundreds killed?” Tian Shu kowtowed again and said, “When Guan Gao conspired, the emperor decreed death for the whole clan of anyone who stayed with King Zhang Ao—yet Meng Shu shaved his head like a convict and followed his lord, ready to die with him. He had no idea he would ever govern Yunzhong! Han and Chu had worn the army to the bone, and Modu had just cowed the northern tribes and turned them on the border. Meng Shu saw how spent his men were and could not bring himself to hold them back; they rushed to the walls and died like sons defending a father. Those hundreds fell of their own zeal—Meng Shu did not drive them to it! That is precisely what makes Meng Shu a man of true honor.” The emperor exclaimed, “Meng Shu is a worthy man indeed!” He recalled Meng Shu and restored him as governor of Yunzhong.
13
後數歲，叔坐法失官。 梁孝王使人殺漢議臣爰盎，景帝召叔案梁，具得其事。 還報，上曰：「梁有之乎？」 對曰：「有之。」 「事安在？」 叔曰：「上無以梁事為問也。 今梁王不伏誅，是廢漢法也； 如其伏誅，太后食不甘味，臥不安席，此憂在陛下。」 於是上大賢之，以為魯相。
Some years later Tian Shu lost his post over a legal matter. When Prince Xiao of Liang had the Han minister Yuan Ang murdered, Emperor Jing sent Tian Shu to investigate the kingdom of Liang, and he uncovered the whole story. On his return the emperor asked, “Was Liang behind it?” Tian Shu said, “Yes.” “Where is the proof?” Tian Shu replied, “Your Majesty should not press for details of Liang’s guilt. If the prince goes unpunished, the law of Han is a dead letter; if he is executed, the empress dowager will lose her appetite and her sleep—the grief will be yours alone.” The emperor was deeply impressed and named him chancellor of Lu.
14
相初至官，民以王取其財物自言者百餘人。 叔取其渠率二千人笞，怒之曰：「王非汝主邪？ 何敢自言主！」 魯王聞之，大慚，發中府錢，使相償之。 相曰：「王自使人償之，不爾，是王為惡而相為善也。」
Soon after he took office, well over a hundred commoners came forward claiming the prince had seized their property. Tian Shu seized their ringleaders—some two thousand men—and had them flogged, shouting, “Is the prince not your sovereign? How dare you bring suit against your sovereign in open court!” The Prince of Lu was mortified, opened the inner treasury, and told the chancellor to pay them off. Tian Shu refused: “Let His Highness send his own men with the money—otherwise it will look as though the prince did wrong and the chancellor bought virtue.”
15
魯王好獵，相常從入苑中，王輒休相就館。 相常暴坐苑外，終不休，曰：「吾王暴露，獨何為舍？」 王以故不大出遊。
The Prince of Lu loved the chase; the chancellor always rode with him into the hunting park, but the prince kept urging him to withdraw to the guest quarters. Tian Shu would sit outside in the weather and refuse to leave, saying, “If my lord endures wind and cold, how may I take shelter alone?” After that the prince rarely went out on long hunts.
16
數年以官卒，魯以百金祠，少子仁不受，曰：「義不傷先人名。」
He died in office some years later; the state of Lu sent a hundred catties of gold for his funeral, but his youngest son Ren declined it, saying, “Duty forbids me to tarnish my father’s reputation.”
17
仁以壯勇為衛將軍舍人，數從擊匈奴。 衛將軍進言仁為郎中，至二千石、丞相長史，失官。 後使刺三河，還，奏事稱意，拜為京輔都尉。 月餘，遷司直。 數歲，戾太子舉兵，仁部閉城門，令太子得亡，坐縱反者族。
Ren’s courage won him a place as a client of General-in-chief Wei Qing, and he rode with him on several campaigns against the Xiongnu. Wei Qing recommended him for gentleman of the palace; he rose to two-thousand-bushel rank and chief clerk under the chancellor, then lost his post. Later he was dispatched to inspect the Three Rivers circuit; his report pleased the throne, and he was named metropolitan commandant for the capital region. A month later he was promoted to imperial rectifier. Years afterward, when the heir apparent Liu Ju rose in arms, Ren’s detachment shut the city gates in a way that let the prince slip away; for abetting a rebel he and his clan were executed.
18
贊曰：以項羽之氣，而季布以勇顯名楚，身履軍搴旗者數矣，可謂壯士。 及至困厄奴僇，苟活而不變，何也？ 彼自負其材，受辱不羞，欲有所用其未足也，故終為漢名將。 賢者誠重其死。 夫婢妾賤人，感概而自殺，非能勇也，其畫無俚之至耳。 欒布哭彭越，田叔隨張敖，赴死如歸，彼誠知所處，雖古烈士，何以加哉！
The historian’s judgment: In an age of Xiang Yu’s fury, Ji Bu made a name in Chu for sheer nerve—time and again he charged the enemy line and tore down their banners—a true fighting man. Yet when they sank to the life of a slave and the disgrace of the collar, they still clung to life instead of throwing it away—why? They trusted their own abilities; they could endure humiliation because they were not finished with their ambitions, and in the end they rose to fame as Han’s great generals. A worthy man weighs his death as he weighs his life. When a bondmaid or some other nobody works herself into a passion and cuts her own throat, that is not bravery—it is the most witless kind of gesture. Luan Bu who mourned Peng Yue, Tian Shu who followed Zhang Ao to the scaffold—they went to death as calmly as homecoming because they knew exactly where they stood; the sternest heroes of antiquity could do no more.
