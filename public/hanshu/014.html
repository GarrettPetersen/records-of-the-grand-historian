In ancient times, the Zhou dynasty drew lessons from the Xia and Shang. The three sage kings established a system of five noble ranks, enfeoffing over eight hundred states, with more than fifty ruled by members of the royal family. The Duke of Zhou was enfeoffed in Lu and Duke Kang in Wei, each domain spanning several hundred square li; The Grand Duke was enfeoffed in Qi, a territory as large as that of five marquises and nine earls. The Book of Odes describes their system: "The noble men serve as protective screens, the great ministers as sturdy walls. Great states serve as shields, great clans as supporting wings. Those who cherish virtue find peace, the royal clansmen form the city defenses. Do not allow the defenses to crumble, do not face this danger alone." This system honored relatives and rewarded the worthy, publicly recognized meritorious deeds, and tied the fate of these noble houses to the dynasty fortunes, creating deep roots and strong foundations that could not be uprooted. In times of prosperity, the Zhou and Shao clans assisted in governance, achieving such harmony that punishments were set aside; in times of decline, the five hegemons supported their weakness and helped defend the realm. After the reigns of Kings You and Ping, the dynasty steadily declined. It was squeezed between the Yellow and Luo Rivers, split into Eastern and Western Zhou, with kings fleeing their duties and suffering the condemnation of usurped authority. Yet the world still recognized it as the common sovereign, and even the strongest powers dared not overthrow it. It endured for over eight hundred years, until its fate was exhausted and virtue depleted. By King Nan's time, it had descended to commoner status and ended with a natural death. Though the Zhou title and position had ended, the remaining branches of the family still propped each other up. No one could claim the vacant throne, leaving the empire without a ruler for over thirty years.

Qin occupied strategically superior territory and deployed cunning, deceitful armies. They gradually devoured the eastern states, achieving victory in every endeavor. Proud of their accustomed ways and trusting only their own wisdom, they ridiculed the three ancient dynasties, destroyed traditional laws, and arrogated the title of emperor for themselves. They made their sons and brothers marquises, but had no close relatives as trusted advisors within, and no territorial buffer as external defense. Chen She and Wu Guang raised their wooden staves in rebellion, and Liu Bang and Xiang Yu followed to destroy the Qin dynasty. Hence the saying: Zhou outlasted its allotted time, Qin fell short of its destiny - such are the fortunes of states.

At the founding of the Han dynasty, the empire was newly unified but the royal family was small. Learning from Qin's disastrous isolation, they divided the territories and established a two-tier system of nobility. Meritorious ministers were enfeoffed as marquises in over a hundred towns. The emperor's sons and brothers were honored, and nine major kingdoms were established. From Yanmen eastward to Liaoyang became the kingdoms of Yan and Dai. South of Mount Changshan, where the Taihang Mountains turn westward, crossing the Yellow River and Ji River to the sea, became the kingdoms of Qi and Zhao. Westward from the Gu and Si Rivers, encompassing Mount Gui and Mount Meng, became the kingdoms of Liang and Chu. To the east bordering the Yangtze and lakes, extending to Kuaiji, became the kingdoms of Jing and Wu. Bordered to the north by the Huai River, encompassing Mount Lu and Mount Heng, became the kingdom of Huainan. South of the Han River, extending to the Jiuyi Mountains, became the kingdom of Changsha. The northern frontiers of these kingdoms encircled the border markets, connecting externally with the Xiongnu and Yue tribes. The emperor directly controlled the commanderies of Sanhe, Dongjun, Yingchuan, and Nanyang. From Jiangling westward to Bashu, and northward from Yunzhong to Longxi, plus the Capital Interior Commandery, made fifteen commanderies in total. Princesses and marquises also held many towns within these areas. Yet the largest feudal kingdoms boasted territories spanning entire provinces and commanderies, controlling dozens of connected cities. Their palaces and officials followed the same system as the imperial capital - one might say they bent the rules beyond what was proper. Nevertheless, when Gaozu founded the dynasty, he was overwhelmed with daily affairs. Emperor Hui's reign was brief, and Empress Gao ruled as regent. Yet the empire remained peaceful, free from rebellious plots. The LÃ¼ clan's usurpation was ultimately thwarted, and Emperor Wen's reign was secured - all thanks to the support of the feudal lords.

However, the feudal lords were originally established as powerful rulers, but later generations abused their power excessively. The smaller ones became licentious and lawless, while the greater ones grew estranged, isolated, and rebellious, ultimately destroying themselves and losing their kingdoms. Therefore Emperor Wen adopted Jia Yi's proposal to divide Qi and Zhao, while Emperor Jing implemented Chao Cuo's plan to reduce the power of Wu and Chu. Emperor Wu implemented Zhu Fu's proposal and issued the Edict of Extended Kindness, allowing feudal lords to divide their territories to enfeoff their sons and brothers. Without formal demotions, the kingdoms gradually fragmented on their own. From this point on, Qi was split into seven parts, Zhao into six, Liang into five, and Huainan into three. When imperial sons were newly enfeoffed as kings, even the largest kingdoms contained no more than a dozen or so cities. Though Changcheng, Yan, and Dai retained their ancient names, they no longer had northern or southern frontiers to defend. Emperor Jing faced the Rebellion of the Seven States, suppressed the feudal lords, and reduced the ranks of their officials. Emperor Wu dealt with the rebellions of Hengshan and Huainan by creating the law of "left officials" and establishing rules for royal succession. Feudal lords could only collect taxes and rents for their living expenses, with no role in governing.
