Emperor Guangwu, whose personal name was Xiu and courtesy name was Wenshu, was posthumously titled Shizu. According to the Book of Rites, the title 'Ancestor' (Zu) is reserved for those of great merit; as he led the Han restoration, his temple name was accordingly designated Shizu. According to the system of posthumous titles: 'Guang' (Shining) denotes one who continues the legacy of their predecessors, while 'Wu' (Martial) signifies one who successfully quells disorder and rebellion. The 'Notes on Past and Present' by Marquis Fu records that Xiu's courtesy name was also known as Mao. The terms Bo, Zhong, Shu, and Ji indicate the birth order of brothers from eldest to youngest. As his eldest brother was Bosheng and his second brother was Zhong, his own courtesy name was designated Wenshu to reflect his position as the third son." Caiyang was a county whose ancient administrative seat lay to the southwest of present-day Zaoyang in Suizhou. (Changsha Commandery corresponds to the area of modern Tanzhou.) Further details are recorded in the Biographies of the Four Kings of the Imperial Clan. The Book of Han (Former Book) states that the office of Commandery Administrator was a position established by the Qin dynasty." The post carried an official rank and salary of 2,000 piculs of grain. During the reign of Emperor Jing, the title of the office was changed to Grand Administrator (Taishou)." The Book of Han (Former Book) states that the office of Military Commandant was originally the Commandery Commandant, a position established by the Qin dynasty." The officer was responsible for assisting the Grand Administrator and managing military affairs, holding a rank equivalent to 2,000 piculs. During the reign of Emperor Jing, the title of the office was changed to Military Commandant (Duwei)." The Book of Han (Former Book) states that the offices of Magistrate (Ling) and Chief (Zhang) were both established during the Qin dynasty." Counties with ten thousand or more households were headed by a Magistrate, whose rank ranged from 1,000 to 600 piculs; those with fewer than ten thousand households were headed by a Chief, whose rank ranged from 500 to 300 piculs." At the age of nine, the future Emperor Guangwu was orphaned and was subsequently raised by his uncle, Liu Liang. He stood seven chi and three cun tall, with a handsome beard and eyebrows, a wide mouth, a high-bridged nose, and a 'sun-horn' protrusion on his forehead. ('Long' signifies a high or prominent feature.) The physiognomist Xu Fu remarked: "The tip of the nose is referred to as the 'Zhun'." Zheng Xuan, in his commentary on the 'Shangshu Zhonghou', explained: "A 'sun-horn' refers to a bony protrusion in the middle of the forehead, shaped like the sun." (Zhong refers to Liu Xi, the Marquis of Heyang, who was known for his skill in managing the family estate.) This is recorded in the Book of Han. (The 'Records of the Eastern Pavilion' notes that he studied the Book of Documents under Xu Ziwei, a Palace Grandee from Lujiang.) Due to a lack of funds, he pooled his resources with his roommate, a student named Han, to purchase a donkey. They then had an attendant hire it out to cover their collective expenses."

In the eleventh month, a comet was sighted in the constellation of Zhang. (The 'Phonetics and Meanings of the Book of Han' describes a comet as having short, bushy rays of light.)
